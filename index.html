The Problem with Weight-Based Racing

In traditional virtual cycling platforms like Zwift, performance is calculated using Watts per Kilogram (W/kg), which creates an unbalanced playing field where body type often matters more than pure cycling power.

The Challenge: A 60kg rider putting out 300W (5.0 W/kg) will easily drop a 90kg rider also putting out 300W (3.3 W/kg) on any significant climb, despite both producing identical power.

Frequently Asked Questions

Wouldn't this system favor heavier riders?

No, it wouldn't favor any body type. It would simply create a level playing field where the rider who produces the most power wins, regardless of their weight. Heavy riders with high power outputs would no longer be penalized on climbs.

How would this work on different virtual cycling platforms?

The Power Pure standard would work the same way across all platforms. Riders would be categorized based on their verified power output, and all platforms would use the same physics model that neutralizes weight and height advantages.

Would climbs still feel like climbs?

Yes! The terrain would still affect your speed - you would need to produce more power to maintain speed on a climb than on flat ground. The difference is that all riders would face the same power requirements for the same speed on any given gradient.

How would category changes work throughout the season?

Your Power Pure category would be dynamically updated based on your recent performance. If your power increases through training, you'd move up. If you're in a recovery period, you'd race in a lower category, ensuring fair competition year-round.

What about different rider heights and aerodynamics?

Power Pure standardizes both weight AND aerodynamics. All riders compete with identical virtual profiles. Your power output is the only variable that affects your performance.
